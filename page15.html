Reading Response 2:
Rachel Greene

I found this article very interesting, especially considering the current state of digital art. In a world of NFTs and sophisticated technology, it’s fascinating to learn about the roots of digital art, and to compare where we started to where we are today. The concept behind “net.art” is intriguing to me, especially the fact that the term stemmed from a simple corrupted email. It’s hard to imagine a world where digital art and physical art were so separate, but in reality it was only 25 years ago. I appreciate how the article took a timeline approach to it’s exploration; I was born at the end of the 90s, so I never really knew what that world was like, year by year. One line in particular that stood out to me, was that in ‘96 people began to realize that internet culture was having a profound impact on our world.

Some other things about this piece surprised me, like jodi.org. I had heard about jodi.org in the past before, but I didn’t realize the full history behind it, like it’s iconic 404 project. It really is impressive to see how influential these things can be, even after all this time. Another thing that interested me was the prevalence of subscription email lists (you know, outside of marketing stuff). I remember their popularity a little bit when I was younger, but I found it neat just how popular they were in the 90s, even for the most niche of subjects.

All in all, I think this is a really cool window into the past, and it really showcased how influential net.art was in the long run.
