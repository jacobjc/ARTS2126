Reading Response 1: David Dunn

What I enjoy so much about Dunn's piece is the contexts he addresses. Firstly, he describes how audio is hardwired into us as humans, in fact even more so than vision. It's part of our instinct. What we've done and experienced over our lifetime and our ancestors' has directly impacted how we understand our world through sound. He goes on to describe how music is just a subset of our auditory experience; a "metaphor" for how we map the world's noise into human emotion. I think this is a strong point, since music can not be created without an existing reference. For example, we use drum beats in music often, but we only know of Earthly drum beats. On Mars, drums could sound different due to atmospheric differences.

Dunn also described our individual contexts. Sure, from person to person, experiences with noise and sound will differ, but that is becoming less so in our digital age. But what about comparing person to animal? Dunn points out that the differences in sound experiences between a human and a lion are so great, that they couldn't describe it to each other even if they spoke the same language. For me, this is driving home the idea that sound is it's own language. Just as we can't describe the color blue without seeing it before, we also can't describe the sound of the beach without prior knowledge.

On a personal note, I believe that the best experiences we can have as humans are ones that combine our sensible languages. Adding visual, audible, and physical layers to express art is what makes for a memorable experience.
